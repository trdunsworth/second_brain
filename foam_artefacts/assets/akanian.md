📖 Akanian: Grammar and Phonology Guidebook
Welcome to the systematic structure of Akanian (the name can be easily replaced or adjusted). This document details the comprehensive rules governing the language's morphology, phonology, and syntax, providing the framework for the full language paradigm.

Ⅰ. Phonological System
Akanian is characterized by a highly regular, agglutinative phonetic structure, prioritizing clarity and ease of learning.

A. Phonemic Inventory
The inventory consists of 5 simple vowels and 17 consonants.

Vowels (V): /a, e, i, o, u/
Consonants (C): /p, b, t, d, k, g, f, v, m, n, l, r, w/ (Original)
Advanced Consonants: /ʃ/ (sh), /tʃ/ (ch), /θ/ (th), /ð/ (dh).
B. Phonotactics Rules
Syllable Structure: Syllables are predominantly (C)V(C). Complex consonant clusters are strictly forbidden.
Stress: Stress is uniform or falls on the root's initial syllable.
Vowel Harmony: Affixes (prefixes and suffixes) are restricted. When attaching to roots, they must follow a predictable harmony rule (e.g., a back vowel suffix must attach to a root containing /a, o, u/).
Ⅱ. Morphological System: Nouns and Nominal Phrases
A. Gender System (Grammatical Agreement)
Every noun belongs to one of three genders, which dictates certain agreement markers (especially for adjectives).

Anima (A): Sentient beings (People, Animals).
Cosmo (C): Natural, abstract concepts (Sky, Time, Idea).
Artif (R): Manufactured, small, non-sentient objects (Tools, Books, Stone).
B. Case System (The 12 Cases)
Cases define the role of a noun phrase within a sentence. These cases are highly agglutinative.

Ⅲ. Morphological System: Verbs
The Akanian verb is perhaps the most complex element, requiring agreement across five dimensions (Person, Number, Tense, Aspect).

A. Tense and Aspect (TA)
Tense defines when the action happens; Aspect defines how the action unfolds.

Tense (T):
$\mathbf{-a}$: Past (Completed action).
$\mathbf{-i}$: Present (Ongoing or habitual state).
$\mathbf{-o}$: Future (Intended action).
Aspect (A):
$\mathbf{-kan}$: Perfective (The action is viewed as a single, completed block).
$\mathbf{-ren}$: Imperfective (The action is viewed as ongoing, descriptive, or habitual).
B. Number and Person Agreement (PN)
These two markers are combined and act as the ultimate suffix of the verb structure.

1. Person Prefixes (The Subject Marker)

These prefixes must be the first element applied after the Tense/Aspect complex.

Person	Marker (Prefix)	Function
1st Person	$\mathbf{mi-}$	Speaker ("I," "We").
2nd Person (Informal)	$\mathbf{ta-}$	Close Listener ("You").
2nd Person (Formal)	$\mathbf{tu-}$	Honored Listener ("You").
3rd Person	$\mathbf{nu-}$	Third party ("He," "She," "They").
2. Number Suffixes (The Quantity Marker)

This suffix follows the person prefix.

Singular	$\mathbf{-a}$	(One)

Ⅳ. Syntactic System (Syntax)
Akanian employs a Topic-Comment structure, making word order extremely flexible, as the grammatical role of every element is determined by case markers and particles.

B. Particles (Grammatical Glue)
Particles are small, function words that attach to or precede a phrase and signal its role in the sentence.

TOP Marker ($\text{wa}$): Establishes the overall topic of the utterance (Thematic Focus). Must mark the topic NP.
CASE Markers: Define the noun’s role (Thematic Role).
Focus Marker ($\text{ko}$): Marks the element that is the most important or newly introduced piece of information (Rhematic Focus).
C. Example of Flexibility
If a speaker wants to emphasize who performed the action, they might move the Subject NP next to the Topic marker, even if the default word order dictates otherwise. The particle system makes this possible.

I. Phonological System
1. Vowel Harmony Ambiguity

Issue: The rule states: "a back vowel suffix must attach to a root containing /a, o, u/."
Critique: This is insufficient. If a root contains /e/ or /i/, does the suffix have to contain front vowels? If a root contains a mix (e.g., ka-me), which rule takes precedence?
Suggestion: Define a strict algorithm. Example: *If root contains any front vowel (-e, -i), suffix must be front (-e, -i). If all back, suffix must be back.* Also, explicitly state whether the root vowels themselves change (e.g., Vowel Harmony usually implies the root changes to match the suffix, not the other way around).
Stress Rule: "Stress is uniform or falls on the root's initial syllable."
Critique: This is contradictory. A syllable cannot be both uniformly stressed and specifically initial-stressed unless it is fixed.
Suggestion: Declare a fixed stress rule (e.g., "Primary stress falls on the first syllable of the root, regardless of suffixes").
2. Consonant Inventory Logic

Issue: The distinction between "Original" (10) and "Advanced" (4) consonants is unclear. /w/ and /r/ are standard consonants in many Indo-European and Afro-Asiatic families, yet they are grouped with the original set while fricatives like /ʃ/ are "advanced."
Critique: Why are /w/ and /r/ included in the original list if they are not "advanced"? Are the advanced consonants phonemes or derived clusters? If they are phonemes, why are they treated as a secondary tier?
Suggestion: Standardize the inventory. Do not segregate phonemes into tiers unless there is a sociolinguistic reason (e.g., loan layer). Remove "Advanced" if they are native phonemes. If they are loan phonemes, specify they do not participate in Vowel Harmony.
II. Morphological System (Nouns & Cases)
1. Case System Load

Issue: The design specifies 12 distinct cases.
Critique: A 12-case system creates high cognitive load and is typologically rare (e.g., Turkish has ~8, Finnish has ~15 but simplified in modern usage). For a "highly regular, agglutinative" language intended for clarity (as stated), 12 cases is excessive. It creates massive memorization overhead without adding significant syntactic value beyond the Nominative/Accusative/Genitive/Dative/Inessive/Elative/Comitative/Superessive/Allative/Adlative/Locative.
Suggestion: Reduce to 6-8 core cases. Merge Superessive into Locative, and Illative into Locative (or distinguish by directional particles rather than distinct case endings). Combine Directional (-si) into the Locative or Ablative depending on movement. This simplifies the paradigm from 12 to ~6.
Redundancy: You state that the Topic NP must be marked with the particle wa. However, if every NP has a case ending, the Topic Marker wa might be redundant. Case endings already identify the subject (Nominative) and object (Accusative).
2. Adjective Agreement

Issue: "All adjectives and descriptive modifiers must agree with the Gender of the noun."
Critique: In a high-case system, adjectives typically agree with Case as well as Gender, not just Gender.
Suggestion: Define if adjectives are invariable or fully declined. If they agree in Gender, do they also take the Case ending? If they are invariable adjectives, how does the listener know the adjective modifies the Topic vs. the Comment?
Risk: If adjectives only agree in Gender, but nouns change Case, you lose the ability to distinguish adjective-noun relationships across sentences. Consider requiring adjectives to decline for the case of the noun they modify, or restrict agreement to Gender only in a Topic-Comment structure (e.g., "The book [C-Case] is red [C-Gender]").
III. Morphological System (Verbs)
1. Structural Contradiction

Issue: Section B states Person Markers are "Prefixes" (mi-, ta-, etc.), but Section C states the structure is: Root + Aspect + Tense + Person Prefix + Number Suffix.
Critique: A prefix cannot be placed after the Tense/Aspect complex. If it comes after the Tense/Aspect, it is an Infix or Suffix, not a Prefix. Furthermore, placing the Person Marker after the Tense/Aspect violates the standard agglutinative hierarchy (Subject usually precedes the verbal stem).
Suggestion: Standardize the structure. Agglutinative verbs typically follow: Person Prefix + Root + Tense/Aspect + Number Suffix.
Agreement Logic: You state verbs must agree with the Gender of the object.
Critique: This conflicts with your heavy Case System. If the Object NP is marked with an Accusative suffix (-l), why does the verb need to agree with the Gender of that object? The case ending already identifies it as a target. Object agreement is typically reserved for pro-drop languages (like Japanese or Bantu) where the noun isn't always stated. Since Akanian has full case-marking, object agreement is redundant.
Suggestion: Remove object gender agreement on verbs. Rely on the Case ending of the NP for syntactic function. If you keep object agreement, reduce the case system to 4-5 cases (Nominative/Accusative/Genitive/Participle), and keep the verb agreement.
2. Conjugation Complexity

Issue: The structure claims to cover $3 \text{ (P)} \times 4 \text{ (N)} \times 2 \text{ (A)} \times 3 \text{ (T)} = 72$ forms.
Critique: While mathematically sound, this creates a massive paradigm.
Suggestion: Check for "Portmanteau Morphemes" (fusion). For example, does ta-i-a mean 2nd Person Singular Past? Or does the number -a merge with the tense -i? Agglutinative languages often merge tense/number markers (like Japanese ta vs u). Consider creating "Tense-Number" portmanteaux to reduce syllable count.
IV. Syntactic System
1. Topic-Comment Redundancy

Issue: The syntax relies on a "Topic-Comment" structure with a specific wa marker for topics.
Critique: If word order is determined by Case Markers (as stated), a "Topic" marker is functionally identical to the Nominative Case ending. Marking the Subject with wa is redundant if the Subject already has the -u (Nominative) suffix.
Suggestion: Remove the wa particle. If the Nominative case (-u) marks the Subject, that is the Topic in a Subject-Predicate structure. Reserve wa for non-verbal predicates (e.g., "The topic of this book is red").
Focus Marker: The Focus Marker (ko) is described as marking new information.
Critique: In Topic-Comment languages, the Focus is usually determined by word order (the element in the Comment position). Adding a ko suffix creates a "Focus-Case" overlap.
Suggestion: Integrate ko into the Verb (e.g., an auxiliary particle) rather than attaching it to the NP, to avoid stacking too many suffixes.
2. Word Order Flexibility

Issue: "The default, most natural order is: Topic → NPs → Predicate."
Critique: This is a Topic-Predicate order (SVO or VSO). But if Case endings are active, you don't need strict word order.
Suggestion: Explicitly state if the Verb is final (SOV) or medial (SVO). If the Verb is final, ensure that Adjectives and Adverbs do not scramble the NPs.
Particles: Ensure wa (Topic) and ko (Focus) are not mutually exclusive with the Case Markers. If a Noun has Case + Focus + Topic, the word becomes unpronounceable. Simplify the particle system.
V. Overall Paradigm Design
1. Homophony and Disambiguation

Issue: If Vowel Harmony is active and there are 12 Case endings, vowel length is not mentioned.
Critique: Without vowel length, you risk homophones. Example: man (Nominative) vs man (Accusative). If you have man (Nominative) and man (Accusative), you need a length distinction or a different consonant.
Suggestion: Introduce Vowel Length distinction (Short a vs Long aa) for Case distinctions to maintain phonotactic balance and disambiguation.
2. Typological Coherence

Issue: The design mixes features from unrelated language families.
Agglutinative morphology (Turkish/Japanese/Finnish).
Topic-Comment structure (Dravidian/Polynesian).
12-Case system (Dyirbal/Nahuatl/Inuit).
Object Gender Agreement (Bantu).
Critique: While possible, this creates a hybrid that lacks internal logic. For example, combining high case marking (Inuit/Dyirbal) with Object Gender Agreement (Bantu) is rare.
Suggestion: Choose a specific typological family.
If Finnish/Turkish: Reduce cases, drop object agreement, keep vowel harmony.
If Dravidian: Keep Topic-Comment, simplify cases, drop object gender agreement.
Recommendation: Lean towards a Finnic/Dravidian hybrid. Keep 12 Cases (as they allow freedom of word order), but remove the Object Gender Agreement on verbs to avoid redundancy with the Case system.
3. Verb Person Prefixes

Issue: The verb prefix order is Root + Aspect + Tense + Person.
Critique: Placing the Person Prefix after the Root (implied by the "Person Prefix" name) breaks standard morpheme ordering.
Suggestion: Move the Person Marker to the beginning of the stem: Person + Root + Aspect + Tense + Number.
Example: nu-root-kan-a-ni-te (He walked).
This prevents the "Prefix after Tense" contradiction.
Summary of Recommendations
Simplify Cases: Merge the 12 cases into 6 distinct semantic roles (Subject, Object, Location, Source, Goal, Possession).
Fix Verb Order: Move the Person Marker to the beginning of the verb stem. Remove the instruction to place Person Prefixes after Tense.
Remove Object Agreement: Rely on Case Endings (-l) for object identification rather than Verb Gender Agreement.
Clarify Harmony: Define the algorithm for Vowel Harmony strictly (Back vs. Front trigger).
Add Length: Consider vowel length to prevent homophony in the absence of tone.
Remove Redundant Particles: Merge wa (Topic) with the Nominative Case ending if the verb marks the predicate.
This structure reduces memorization overhead while maintaining the agglutinative clarity you sought. It creates a more consistent typological profile (likely Afro-Asiatic or Finno-Ugric influence) rather than a random hybrid.
